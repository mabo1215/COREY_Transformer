\section*{Part 1: Review Summary}

\subsection*{Summary:}  
This paper introduces COREY, a prototype framework designed to optimize memory-bandwidth limitations in Mamba-1.x Selective State Space Models (SSMs). The core innovation lies in using \textbf{activation entropy} (Signal A), estimated via lightweight fixed-width histograms, as a runtime signal to dynamically select chunk sizes and define kernel fusion boundaries. The authors evaluate the mechanism across two tiers: a deterministic Python cost model (Tier 1) and a real-checkpoint GPU feasibility study using Mamba-370M/1.4B (Tier 2). The paper also provides a theoretical analysis of Hadamard reparameterization as a method for outlier suppression and entropy enhancement.

\subsection*{Strengths:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Novelty of Signal:} Using activation entropy as a prompt-responsive scheduling signal is a creative departure from traditional static graph-level fusion or profile-based tuning [1, 2].
    \item \textbf{Theoretical Rigor:} The paper provides three formal theorems (Entropy Growth, Fusion Depth Bound, and Quantization Stability) that link distributional geometry to systems-level hardware constraints [3-5].
    \item \textbf{Hardware Awareness:} The scheduler explicitly accounts for register pressure, shared memory usage, and occupancy, making the "fusion score" grounded in physical hardware limits [6, 7].
    \item \textbf{Transparency:} The authors are exceptionally clear about the scope of their claims, explicitly stating what is \textit{not} claimed (e.g., end-to-end speedup of a full fused kernel or perplexity improvements under Hadamard) [8, 9].
    \item \textbf{Empirical Results:} Demonstrated a $3.24\times$ latency reduction at the kernel level on real RTX 3070/3090 hardware by automatically recovering optimal chunk sizes [10].
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Major Weaknesses and Concerns}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Roman*)]
      \item \textbf{Implementation Gap:} The primary system contribution (entropy-guided fusion) is not yet integrated as a full fused kernel in the inference path. The hook is currently "passive" on real checkpoints [9, 11].
    \item \textbf{Limited Workload Diversity:} The real-workload evaluation is confined to 80 prompts from a LongBench subset. The results show that entropy is tightly concentrated, meaning the dynamic scheduler effectively acts as a stable auto-tuner rather than a rapidly switching controller in this specific regime [12].
    \item \textbf{Hadamard Disconnect:} There is a significant gap between the synthetic "heavy-tailed" regime (where Hadamard increases entropy) and real Mamba checkpoints (where entropy decreases after rotation) [13, 14]. This limits the current practical utility of Theorem 1.
    \item \textbf{Baseline Comparisons:} The evaluation lacks comparisons with more recent architectures like Mamba-2 or contemporary Transformers (e.g., with FlashAttention-3) at similar scales [15].
    \item \textbf{Implementation Gap (The "Passive Hook" Issue):} For a TPAMI-level systems paper, the current integration is insufficient. The scheduler hook is currently "passive"—it suggests a tile size but does not yet forward it to the model execution. A "closed-loop" end-to-end evaluation is required to substantiate the claimed performance gains in a production-like environment. [9, 10]
    \item \textbf{Disjoint between Theory and Real-World Activations:} Theorem 1 (Entropy Growth) is empirically falsified on real Mamba checkpoints, where entropy actually decreases after Hadamard rotation. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper's core motivation—that entropy serves as a "safety signal" for fusion—is weakened if the theory only holds for synthetic "heavy-tailed" data. [7, 11]
    \item \textbf{Insufficient Competitive Baselines:} The experimental section lacks comparisons with state-of-the-art SSMs (Mamba-2/SSD) and optimized Transformers (FlashAttention-3). To satisfy TPAMI standards, the authors must demonstrate that COREY offers advantages over static profile-tuned memory managers in a wider architectural context. [12, 13]
    \item \textbf{Generalization of the Entropy Signal:} The 80 real LongBench prompts exhibit a very narrow entropy range, causing the scheduler to "collapse" into a single chunk bucket (256). This suggests COREY currently acts as a static auto-tuner rather than a truly dynamic prompt-responsive controller. [14, 15]
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Part 2: Rating and Confidence}

\textbf{Overall Score:} 6 (Marginal Accept) \\
\textbf{Confidence Score:} 4 (High)

\textbf{Justification:} While the paper presents a "prototype study," the conceptual framework and the alignment between information theory (entropy) and systems optimization (fusion/tiling) are strong. The kernel-level timing results on real GPUs provide sufficient "proof of concept." However, the lack of end-to-end implementation prevents a higher score.

\section*{Part 3: Questions for Authors}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Overhead of Active Mode:} Based on the analytic budget ($<1\%$), why has the "closing of the loop" (forwarding the chunk size to the kernel) not been implemented? Is there a technical blocker in the Triton interface? [16]
    \item \textbf{Entropy Decrease:} Why do real Mamba activations exhibit an entropy decrease under Hadamard rotation? Is this purely due to the distributions being approximately normal rather than heavy-tailed? [17, 18]
    \item \textbf{Heterogeneous Workloads:} Could the authors provide one example of a real-world activation regime (e.g., code vs. prose) where COREY would actually switch between the 64 and 512 chunk buckets? [19]
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Part 4: Suggestions for Revision}

\subsection*{Mandatory Modifications for Revision}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Close the Loop:} To move toward a "deployment-grade" claim, implement the active forwarding of the tile recommendation to the \texttt{selective\_scan\_fn} and report end-to-end latency .
    \item \textbf{Expand Baselines:} Include a comparison with Mamba-2 (SSD) to demonstrate why entropy guidance is specifically required for Mamba-1.x's hardware characteristics [20].
    \item \textbf{Harmonize Thresholds:} Re-calibrate $H_{ref}$ to $\log K$ as suggested in Appendix A.16 to eliminate bin-count sensitivity and close the 35\% gap to the static-oracle [21].
    \item \textbf{Clarify Hadamard Scope:} Given the findings in Remark 1, suggest moving the Hadamard analysis to a "Discussion" section to focus the "Results" section purely on the validated entropy scheduling mechanism [22].
    \item \textbf{Diversify Prompts:} Test on a more heterogeneous corpus (e.g., interleaving sparse-token code with dense narrative) to validate the "rapid switching" capability of the scheduler [23].
    \item \textbf{Active Loop Integration:} Implement the "Active Mode" where the entropy-derived chunk size is fed directly into the \texttt{selective\_scan\_fn} kernel during real inference. Report end-to-end wall-clock latency improvements across Mamba-370M and 1.4B.
    \item \textbf{Harmonization of $H_{ref}$:} As noted in Appendix A.16, the default $H_{ref}=8.0$ nats is biased. Recalibrate the scheduler using the principled $H_{ref} = \log K$ to close the 35\% gap to the static oracle. [16, 17]
    \item \textbf{Theoretical Refinement:} Provide a more comprehensive analysis of why real Mamba activations exhibit "entropy contraction" rather than growth under Hadamard rotation. Reconcile this with the "Quantization Stability" (Theorem 3) to show if stability increases even when entropy decreases. [7, 18]
    \item \textbf{Heterogeneous Workload Benchmarking:} Conduct experiments on a mixed corpus (e.g., interleaving repetitive log data with dense narrative text) to demonstrate the scheduler's ability to switch chunk buckets in real-time. [19, 20]
\end{enumerate}
