\section*{Summary of the Paper}
%==============================================================================
The authors propose COREY, a runtime scheduler that uses histogram-estimated
Shannon entropy of SSM activations to select kernel chunk sizes for the
Mamba-1.x \texttt{selective\_scan\_fn} Triton kernel. Evidence is organized in
three tiers: (Tier 1) a deterministic Python cost-model prototype;
(Tier 2a) an active inline hook on
\texttt{MambaMixer.cuda\_kernels\_forward} measuring $8.3\%$ end-to-end
overhead on Mamba-370M; (Tier 2b) a direct Triton kernel-timing study showing
$3.24\times$ (default) and $4.41\times$ (calibrated $H_{\text{ref}}=\log K$)
latency reduction over static chunk-64. A prospective Hadamard
reparameterization is analyzed theoretically but explicitly \emph{not} claimed
as a validated contribution; the authors report that Theorem 2 is empirically
falsified on 160/160 real Mamba-370M activation pairs.

\section{Strengths}
%==============================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Honest self-scoping.} The ``Not claimed'' bullet list in the
  introduction and the explicit Tier-1/2a/2b separation are exemplary. Many
  systems submissions overstate kernel microbenchmarks as end-to-end speedups;
  this paper does not.
  \item \textbf{Self-falsification of Theorem 2.} Reporting that the
  doubly-stochastic mixing assumption fails on 160/160 real checkpoint pairs
  (mean $-1.40$ nats) with a mechanistic explanation (Gaussian in\_proj
  outputs are rotation-invariant) is a genuine contribution to the field's
  understanding of Hadamard-based SSM quantization.
  \item \textbf{Principled calibration insight.} The observation that
  $H_{\text{ref}}=\log K$ yields $K$-invariant behavior and closes the gap to
  the one-time-profile oracle (Table~16, Table~17) is a clean result with
  immediate practical value.
  \item \textbf{Tier-2a real-checkpoint feasibility.} The active-mode hook on
  \texttt{MambaMixer.cuda\_kernels\_forward} with measured $8.3\%$ overhead
  ($n{=}5$, isolated per-call cost $1.10 \pm 0.16$\,ms) and observed per-layer
  chunk switching (322 calls chunk=256, 14 calls chunk=128) is credible
  evidence that the mechanism executes on the critical path.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility posture.} Anonymous repository, explicit
  hyperparameters, per-policy timing seeds, and the Sinkhorn-fit residuals in
  Appendix C.1 exceed typical systems-paper disclosure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Major Weaknesses}
\label{sec:weaknesses}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{W1 --- The Tier-2a/2b gap is the central contribution gap.}
The paper demonstrates (a) that entropy selection can be computed inline at
$8.3\%$ overhead and (b) that the selected chunk size \emph{would} produce a
$3.24$--$4.41\times$ kernel-level speedup --- but never connects (a) to (b).
The end-to-end integrated speedup is therefore \emph{unmeasured}, not merely
``deferred.'' For a systems contribution this is the load-bearing
experiment. Routing \texttt{chunk\_size} into \texttt{selective\_scan\_fn} is
one function-signature change; the fact that it was not executed within the
submission window materially weakens the ``Concept \& Feasibility'' framing.

\subsection{W2 --- The prompt-regime concentration undermines the online-adaptation value proposition.}
Table~13 shows all 80 LongBench prompts map to a single coarse chunk bucket
(256) with entropy $4.02 \pm 0.09$ nats. Under the calibrated
$H_{\text{ref}}=\log K$ setting, this is \emph{identical} to what a one-time
offline sweep produces. The authors acknowledge this as ``case (c)'' in
\S6.2, but then the operational advantage reduces to ``no offline sweep
required,'' which is a modest engineering convenience, not a research
contribution. A heterogeneous workload that actually triggers chunk switching
on real prompts is necessary to justify the online mechanism.

\subsection{W3 --- ``No-Fusion'' baseline is a Python dispatch-cost strawman.}
The $37$--$49\times$ speedups against \texttt{policy\_off} (Appendix
Table~21) measure Python-loop overhead, not kernel arithmetic. The authors
are commendably honest about this (footnote $\ddagger$), but the numbers
remain in the paper. A reader skimming the tables will substantially
overestimate COREY's contribution. The fair comparison is
COREY vs.\ Static-512 (calibrated: parity; default: $0.68\times$, i.e.,
\emph{slower}).

\subsection{W4 --- External-baseline coverage is one model (Pythia-410M).}
One Pythia checkpoint as a ``sanity-check'' is insufficient. A Mamba-2 SSD
baseline or a FlashAttention-3 Transformer at matched parameter count would
establish whether the targeted $3.24\times$ (relative to Static-64, not the
oracle) is competitive with alternative architectures solving the same
long-context problem. Without this, the kernel-level speedup is in a vacuum.

\subsection{W5 --- Tier-1 prototype tables consume disproportionate space.}
The prototype surrogate latency tables (Tables~3--10) are deterministic
Python outputs; the authors note the variance is $\pm 0.12$\,ms ``because the
cost model is deterministic.'' These add little over a paragraph of prose.
Reallocating this space to W1/W2 experiments would strengthen the paper.

\subsection{W6 --- The MF-EN exact-match column of Table~22 is uninformative.}
All zeros across all models. The authors acknowledge this is a harness
artifact of 32-token generation vs.\ strict EM, but it still occupies a
column in the main-text-adjacent appendix table. Either replace with
token-F1 / ROUGE-L for MF-EN or remove.

  \item Under the calibrated $H_{\text{ref}}=\log K$ setting, is there

  \item The per-call scheduler cost of $1.10$\,ms (line 297) is $\sim 20\times$
  the $\sim 50\,\mu$s CUDA estimate (line 759). Where is the remaining time
  spent --- Python dispatch, \texttt{cudaDeviceSynchronize}, histogram

  clarify whether the $8.3\%$ overhead can be driven substantially lower.
  \item Theorem 3 ($\ell_\infty$ reduction by $\sqrt{d}$) is stated for the
  sparse-outlier regime and caveated for $D=\Theta(d)$. What is the
  empirical outlier density $D/d$ on real Mamba-370M in\_proj outputs? This
  determines whether Theorem 3 is vacuous or tight for the architecture you
  target.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{abstract}
Mamba-1.x selective state space models (SSMs) offer linear-time sequence
modeling but are memory-bandwidth limited in practice: selective state
updates are decomposed into fragmented kernels with repeated intermediate
tensor materialization. We present COREY, a prototype scheduler that uses
activation entropy---estimated via fixed-width histograms---as a runtime
signal for chunk-size selection at the kernel-invocation granularity.
We position this work as a \emph{Concept \& Feasibility} contribution:
COREY is a single-parameter runtime auto-tuner over an existing Triton
selective-scan kernel, not a new fused kernel.
%
Evidence is organized in three tiers. \textbf{Tier 1} (Python cost model):
entropy-guided grouping reduces surrogate latency and DRAM traffic.
\textbf{Tier 2a} (real-checkpoint inline hook): on Mamba-370M (RTX 3070,
$n{=}5$), entropy computation and chunk selection execute inline on the
critical path of \texttt{model.generate()}; measured end-to-end overhead
is $8.3\%$ for all-48-layer instrumentation, estimated $\approx\!2\%$ with
every-4th-layer sampling (not measured).
\textbf{Tier 2b} (kernel-level scan benchmark): under the principled
calibration $H_{\text{ref}}{=}\log K$, COREY selects the same chunk as
the one-time-profile oracle ($4.41\times$ vs.\ static chunk-64) without
an offline sweep; under the legacy default $H_{\text{ref}}{=}8.0$ it
achieves $3.24\times$.
%
\textbf{What this paper does not claim:} an end-to-end integrated run
that connects Tier 2a (hook) with Tier 2b (chunk-routed scan); this is
the most important future work. Across 80 real LongBench prompts entropy
is tightly concentrated, so present real-workload evidence supports
COREY as a stable auto-tuner within one regime rather than as a
prompt-switching controller. Hadamard reparameterization is analyzed as
a prospective low-bit extension only; we report that Theorem~2 is
empirically falsified on 160/160 real Mamba-370M activation pairs.
Code and data: \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/COREY_Transformer-B0C5/}.

\section*{Patch 2 --- Main-text Theorem-2 falsification paragraph (addresses R8)}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Insert after line~166 in \S4, immediately before \S4.1:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph{Empirical status of Theorem~2 on real checkpoints.}
Theorem~2 gives a sufficient condition---doubly-stochastic histogram
mixing---under which Shannon entropy grows after Hadamard rotation.
We report that this condition is \emph{empirically falsified} on real
Mamba-370M \texttt{in\_proj} outputs: across 160 checkpoint pairs, the
post-Hadamard histogram entropy \emph{decreases} in 160/160 cases
(mean $\Delta H = -1.40 \pm 0.37$ nats). The mechanistic reason is
that real \texttt{in\_proj} outputs are approximately Gaussian, and
orthogonal transforms preserve the Gaussian distribution, so no
histogram spreading occurs. Consequently, while Theorem~2 holds in the
synthetic heavy-tailed regime exercised by our prototype, it should
\emph{not} be used to motivate Hadamard pre-rotation on standard
Mamba-1.x checkpoints. This negative result motivates our decision
to restrict the validated system contribution of this paper to the
entropy-guided chunk scheduler, treating Hadamard as a prospective
extension only (see Appendix~C.1, Remark~1).
\end{verbatim}
