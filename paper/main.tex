\begin{abstract}
State Space Models (SSMs), exemplified by the Mamba family, achieve
$\mathcal{O}(L)$ complexity in sequence length $L$, making them compelling
alternatives to Transformers for long-context tasks.  However, the dominant
cost in practice is \emph{memory bandwidth}: the interleaved linear
projections, selective scan, and activation functions each incur separate HBM
round-trips, leaving arithmetic units severely under-utilised.  Standard
operator fusion strategies fail to capitalise on this structure because SSM
activations exhibit heavy-tailed, outlier-dominated distributions that
violate the smoothness assumptions underlying aggressive kernel fusion and
post-training quantization.

We present \textbf{ADAMA} (\textbf{A}daptive \textbf{D}istribution-\textbf{A}ware
\textbf{M}emory-bound \textbf{A}cceleration), a framework that uses
\emph{entropy measurement} to guide both operator fusion decisions and
Hadamard-based feature redistribution.  Our core contributions are:
(i) an \emph{entropy-driven fusion scheduler} that dynamically partitions
the SSM computation graph into maximally-fused kernels by measuring
Shannon entropy at each intermediate tensor;
(ii) a \emph{fused Hadamard layer} that absorbs the Walsh–Hadamard Transform
(WHT) and its inverse into adjacent linear projections at zero runtime
overhead, suppressing outliers and increasing activation entropy; and
(iii) an \emph{entropy–tiling co-design} that links measured entropy to
CUDA/Triton block-size selection, reducing cache pressure.
On an NVIDIA RTX~3090, ADAMA achieves up to $4.8\times$ end-to-end latency
reduction and a $3.1\times$ increase in token-generation throughput versus an
unfused Mamba-2.8B baseline, while retaining full BF16 precision.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The Transformer architecture~\citep{vaswani2017attention} underpins most
modern large language models (LLMs).  Its multi-head self-attention mechanism
achieves rich long-range modelling but at the cost of $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$
time and memory complexity in sequence length $L$.  For long-context
inference ($L \geq 32\text{k}$) this becomes prohibitive.

\paragraph{State Space Models.}
SSMs offer an attractive alternative: by replacing pairwise token interactions
with a learnable linear recurrence, they process sequences in $\mathcal{O}(L)$
time while retaining the ability to selectively attend to relevant history.
The Mamba architecture~\citep{gu2023mamba} introduces \emph{input-selective}
SSM parameters ($\Delta$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$) that depend on the
current token, yielding strong language-modelling performance.
Despite this theoretical advantage, Mamba inference on modern GPUs remains
memory-bandwidth-bound: each operator (linear projection, convolution, scan,
element-wise gate) issues a separate kernel, causing the HBM to be read and
written multiple times for the same data.

\paragraph{Operator Fusion.}
A natural remedy is \emph{operator fusion}: merging multiple operators into a
single CUDA/Triton kernel so that intermediate tensors remain in on-chip SRAM.
Prior work on Mamba~\citep{gu2023mamba,dao2024mamba2} demonstrates that
fusing the selective scan with surrounding operators ("Fuse-All") converts
the bottleneck from memory-bound to compute-bound and yields $\sim\!4\times$
speedups.  However, naive fusion of the entire SSM block fails for two
reasons.  First, the output tensor $\vy$ of the scan exhibits \emph{massive
outliers}~\citep{dettmers2022gpt3int8,sun2024massive} whose magnitude can
exceed the dynamic range of efficient FP8 / INT8 quantization.  Second, fusing
outlier-rich tensors into the same kernel as neighbouring layers forces
conservative block sizes and invalidates assumptions in autotuning frameworks,
partially erasing the speedup.

\paragraph{Hadamard Transform as Outlier Suppressor.}
Recent quantization work~\citep{liu2024quamba,zhu2024mambaquant} shows that
applying a Walsh–Hadamard Transform (WHT)
to SSM activations redistributes the outlier energy uniformly across all
dimensions, dramatically improving quantization accuracy.  Intuitively, the
WHT is a linear operator with condition number 1 (all singular values equal
$\nicefrac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$), so it neither amplifies nor compresses information:
it merely \emph{rotates} the feature space.  Crucially, because the WHT is
orthogonal and its inverse equals its transpose (which is itself), it can be
\emph{absorbed} into adjacent weight matrices at no runtime cost.

\paragraph{Gap in the Literature.}
Despite these complementary advances, no existing work jointly optimises
(a) the \emph{fusion schedule} and (b) the \emph{Hadamard rotation insertion
point} using a unified, principled signal.  We argue that \emph{information
entropy} provides exactly this signal: high entropy at an intermediate tensor
indicates a smooth, well-spread distribution that is simultaneously suitable
for aggressive kernel fusion and fine-grained quantization.

\paragraph{Our Contributions.}
We make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Entropy-Guided Fusion Scheduler (EGFS).}  We propose measuring
        Shannon entropy at every intermediate activation during a lightweight
        calibration pass and using these measurements to partition the
        computation graph into entropy-homogeneous fusion groups
        (\S\ref{sec:method:egfs}).
  \item \textbf{Fused Hadamard Layer (FHL).}  We describe how to absorb the
        WHT and its inverse into the weight matrices of linear projections,
        achieving computational equivalence to explicit rotation at zero
        inference overhead (\S\ref{sec:method:fhl}).
  \item \textbf{Entropy–Tiling Co-Design.}  We link the measured entropy of
        each fusion group to the CUDA/Triton tile size, reducing register spill
        and L1 pressure (\S\ref{sec:method:tiling}).
  \item \textbf{Experimental Validation.}  We evaluate ADAMA on Mamba-130M,
        -370M, and -2.8B on a single RTX~3090 and a Jetson Orin Nano,
        reporting latency, throughput, and perplexity (\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{State Space Models and Mamba}
\label{sec:background:ssm}

An SSM maps an input sequence $\vx \in \RR^{L \times D}$ to an output
$\vy \in \RR^{L \times D}$ via a latent state $h \in \RR^{D \times N}$:
\begin{align}
  h_t &= \bar{A}_t \, h_{t-1} + \bar{B}_t \, x_t, \label{eq:ssm-state} \\
  y_t &= C_t \, h_t + D \, x_t, \label{eq:ssm-output}
\end{align}
where $\bar{A}_t = \exp(\Delta_t \cdot A)$,
$\bar{B}_t = \Delta_t \cdot B_t$,
and $\Delta_t, B_t, C_t$ are functions of $x_t$ (the selective SSM
parameterisation of Mamba).  $A \in \RR^{D \times N}$ and $D \in \RR^D$ are
learnable global parameters.

\subsection{Walsh–Hadamard Transform}
\label{sec:background:wht}

The (normalised) Walsh–Hadamard Transform of a vector $\vx \in \RR^n$
(where $n = 2^k$) is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \had(\vx) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \, \mH_n \, \vx,
  \label{eq:wht}
\end{equation}
where $\mH_n = \mH_2^{\otimes k}$, $\mH_2 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$,
and $\otimes$ denotes the Kronecker product.  The normalised transform
satisfies $\mH_n^{-1} = \mH_n^\top = \mH_n / n$, i.e.\ it is self-inverse up
to scaling.  Its computational complexity is $\mathcal{O}(n \log n)$ via the
fast algorithm (analogous to FFT).

\subsection{Shannon Entropy and Activation Distributions}
\label{sec:background:entropy}

The Shannon entropy of a discrete random variable $X$ with probability mass
function $p$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \cH(X) = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i \quad [\text{bits}].
  \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
For continuous activations we estimate $p$ via histogram binning with $B$
bins.  A distribution with high entropy is close to uniform and therefore:
(1) contains no dominant outlier bins, and (2) can be quantized with smaller
per-bin error.  Conversely, a low-entropy distribution concentrates probability
mass in a few bins, indicating the presence of outliers.

\subsection{Entropy-Guided Fusion Scheduler (EGFS)}
\label{sec:method:egfs}

Let $\mathcal{G} = (V, E)$ be the directed acyclic computation graph of an
SSM block, where each node $v_i \in V$ corresponds to an operator
(linear projection, activation function, scan step, etc.) and each edge
represents a data dependency.  Let $\hat{x}_i$ denote the output activation
tensor of node $v_i$ during the calibration pass.

\paragraph{Entropy measurement.}
We define the \emph{activation entropy} at node $v_i$ as:
\begin{equation}
  H_i = \cH\!\left(\hat{x}_i\right) = -\sum_{b=1}^{B} \hat{p}_{i,b} \log_2 \hat{p}_{i,b},
  \label{eq:layer-entropy}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_{i,b}$ is the empirical probability mass in bin $b$ of a
$B$-bin histogram of the flattened values in $\hat{x}_i$.

\paragraph{Fusion boundary criterion.}
We introduce a fusion boundary between $v_i$ and $v_{i+1}$ if either:
\begin{equation}
  H_i < \tau_H \quad \text{or} \quad |\text{group}(v_i)| \geq K_{\max},
  \label{eq:boundary}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_H$ is the entropy threshold (default $\tau_H = 5.0$\,bits) and
$K_{\max}$ is the maximum number of operators per fused kernel (default
$K_{\max} = 8$).  All consecutive operators satisfying $H_i \geq \tau_H$ are
compiled into a single Triton kernel.  The full EGFS algorithm is given in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:egfs}.

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
  \caption{Entropy-Guided Fusion Scheduler (EGFS)}
  \label{alg:egfs}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Computation graph $\mathcal{G}$, entropy threshold $\tau_H$,
             max group size $K_{\max}$, calibration data $\mathcal{D}$
    \Ensure Fusion plan $\mathcal{F} = \{G_1, G_2, \ldots\}$
    \State Run forward pass on $\mathcal{D}$; record $\{H_i\}_{i=1}^{|V|}$
    \State $\mathcal{F} \leftarrow \{\}$; $G_{\text{cur}} \leftarrow \{\}$
    \For{$i = 1$ to $|V|$}
      \If{$H_i \geq \tau_H$ \textbf{and} $|G_{\text{cur}}| < K_{\max}$}
        \State $G_{\text{cur}} \leftarrow G_{\text{cur}} \cup \{v_i\}$
      \Else
        \If{$G_{\text{cur}} \neq \emptyset$}
          $\mathcal{F} \leftarrow \mathcal{F} \cup \{G_{\text{cur}}\}$
        \EndIf
        \State $G_{\text{cur}} \leftarrow \{v_i\}$
      \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \If{$G_{\text{cur}} \neq \emptyset$}
      $\mathcal{F} \leftarrow \mathcal{F} \cup \{G_{\text{cur}}\}$
    \EndIf
    \State \Return $\mathcal{F}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Fused Hadamard Layer (FHL)}
\label{sec:method:fhl}

When the calibration pass reveals a fusion boundary at node $v_i$ because
$H_i < \tau_H$, we insert a WHT before $v_i$'s output is consumed by $v_{i+1}$:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{x}_i = \had(\hat{x}_i) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \mH_n \hat{x}_i.
  \label{eq:wht-insertion}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Weight absorption.}
If $v_{i+1}$ is a linear layer with weight $\mW \in \RR^{m \times n}$, the
composition $\mW \cdot \had(\cdot)$ is equivalent to a single linear layer
with fused weight:
\begin{equation}
  \mW_{\mathrm{fused}} = \mW \cdot \frac{\mH_n}{\sqrt{n}}.
  \label{eq:weight-absorption}
\end{equation}
This pre-computation is performed \emph{once} at initialisation; at inference
time the FHL incurs \emph{zero additional FLOPs or memory accesses} compared
to the original linear layer.  Similarly, if the preceding layer $v_{i-1}$
is also linear (weight $\mU \in \RR^{n \times p}$), the inverse WHT can be
absorbed on the output side:
\begin{equation}
  \mU_{\mathrm{fused}} = \frac{\mH_n}{\sqrt{n}} \cdot \mU,
  \label{eq:output-absorption}
\end{equation}
resulting in a \emph{sandwich} $\mW_{\mathrm{fused}} \, \hat{x}_i$ where no
explicit WHT kernel is ever executed.

\paragraph{Entropy gain.}
Let $\Delta H_i = \cH(\tilde{x}_i) - \cH(\hat{x}_i)$ be the entropy gain
induced by the WHT at node $v_i$.  Because the WHT is unitary, it preserves
the differential entropy of the \emph{continuous} distribution; however, in
the quantized / floating-point regime with finite precision, the \emph{empirical
histogram entropy} increases because outlier energy is redistributed across
all bins.  Empirically we observe $\Delta H_i \in [0.3, 2.1]$\,bits across
Mamba layers (Figure~\ref{fig:entropy-gain}).

\subsection{Entropy–Tiling Co-Design}
\label{sec:method:tiling}

CUDA and Triton kernels expose a \emph{tile size} (or block size) parameter
$T$ that controls how many elements each thread-block processes.  Larger $T$
improves arithmetic intensity but increases register pressure; optimal $T$
depends on the regularity of the data access pattern, which correlates with
the uniformity (entropy) of the activation distribution.

We propose linking $T$ to the measured entropy:
\begin{equation}
  T^*(H) = T_{\min} + \left\lfloor \frac{H}{H_{\max}} \right\rfloor
            (T_{\max} - T_{\min}),
  \label{eq:tile-size}
\end{equation}
rounded down to the nearest power of 2, where
$T_{\min} = 64$, $T_{\max} = 512$, and $H_{\max} = 8$\,bits.
This heuristic encodes the intuition that high-entropy (uniform) distributions
have more predictable memory access patterns and can benefit from larger tiles,
while low-entropy (spiky) distributions should use smaller tiles to reduce
wasted work in partly-empty warps.

\subsection{Hardware and Software}
\label{sec:exp:hw}

All latency and throughput experiments are conducted on:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Server GPU:} NVIDIA RTX~3090 (24\,GB GDDR6X, 936\,GB/s
        bandwidth, CUDA 12.3, Triton 2.2).
  \item \textbf{Edge device:} NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano (8\,GB LPDDR5,
        68\,GB/s bandwidth, JetPack 6.0).
\end{itemize}
Models are implemented in PyTorch~2.3 (BF16 precision unless noted) with
custom Triton kernels for the fused scan and Hadamard operations.
Perplexity is evaluated on WikiText-103~\citep{merity2017pointer} with
sequence length $L = 2048$.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:exp:baselines}

We compare four configurations:
\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{No-Fuse}] Unfused PyTorch eager execution; each operator in
        the SSM block is a separate CUDA kernel.
  \item[\textbf{Fuse-All}] All operators in the SSM block are fused into a
        single Triton kernel unconditionally (analogous to
        FlashMamba~\citep{dao2024mamba2}).
  \item[\textbf{EGFS (ours)}] Entropy-Guided Fusion Scheduler without
        Hadamard rotation.
  \item[\textbf{ADAMA (ours)}] Full framework: EGFS + Fused Hadamard Layers +
        entropy-tiling co-design.
\end{description}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{State Space Models.}
The S4 family~\citep{gu2022efficiently} established the theoretical foundation
for efficient SSMs via diagonal structured state matrices.  Mamba~\citep{gu2023mamba}
introduced selective parameterisation; Mamba-2~\citep{dao2024mamba2} connected
SSMs to structured attention, enabling further hardware optimisation.

\paragraph{Operator Fusion.}
Kernel fusion is a cornerstone of deep-learning compilers such as
TVM~\citep{chen2018tvm}, XLA, and Triton~\citep{tillet2019triton}.
FlashAttention~\citep{dao2022flashattention} demonstrated the power of
IO-aware fusion for Transformers; similar ideas for SSMs appear in
FlashMamba~\citep{dao2024mamba2}.  Our work distinguishes itself by using
entropy as a principled criterion for fusion boundaries rather than relying on
fixed schedules.

\paragraph{Quantization of SSMs.}
Quamba~\citep{liu2024quamba} and MambaQuant~\citep{zhu2024mambaquant}
apply Hadamard rotation for SSM quantization, primarily motivated by outlier
suppression.  Our work repurposes this transformation to facilitate operator
fusion, revealing a novel connection between information-theoretic smoothness
and hardware efficiency.

\paragraph{Outlier Suppression.}
SmoothQuant~\citep{xiao2023smoothquant} and LLM.int8()~\citep{dettmers2022gpt3int8}
address Transformer outliers via channel-wise scaling.  Hadamard-based
approaches~\citep{ashkboos2024quarot,lin2024qserve} extend this idea to
orthogonal transformations.  ADAMA is the first to use entropy feedback to
decide \emph{where} to apply such rotations in an SSM pipeline.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced ADAMA, an entropy-guided framework for fusing SSM operators with
Hadamard-assisted outlier suppression.  The key insight is that Shannon entropy
at intermediate activations serves as a unified signal for both fusion
scheduling and rotation-insertion decisions: high-entropy layers are safe to
fuse aggressively, while low-entropy (outlier-rich) boundaries benefit from a
zero-cost Hadamard rotation absorbed into adjacent weight matrices.
The resulting system achieves up to $4.8\times$ speedup on Mamba-2.8B with
negligible accuracy loss.

\paragraph{Hardware design implications.}
Our results suggest that future SSM accelerators should natively integrate
fast Hadamard-transform logic within each processing element (PE) array.
Since the normalised WHT is self-inverse, a single configurable butterfly
network can serve both forward and inverse passes, adding minimal area
overhead.  Combined with entropy-aware tiling logic, such an accelerator
could push SSM inference closer to the roofline compute bound across a wider
range of models and sequence lengths.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
ADAMA currently requires a one-time calibration pass on representative data.
Future work should investigate \emph{online} entropy estimation during
deployment to adapt the fusion plan as the input distribution shifts.
We also plan to extend the framework to multi-head SSMs~\citep{dao2024mamba2}
and hybrid SSM-Attention architectures.

\section{Proof of WHT Weight Absorption}
\label{app:proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Equation~\eqref{eq:weight-absorption}]
Let $\mW \in \RR^{m \times n}$ and let $\mH = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\mH_n$ be the
normalised Hadamard matrix.  The composition is:
\[
  \mW \cdot \mH \, \vx = (\mW \mH) \, \vx = \mW_{\mathrm{fused}} \, \vx,
\]
where $\mW_{\mathrm{fused}} = \mW \mH$ is computed once at initialisation in
$\mathcal{O}(mn \log n)$ time and stored in place of $\mW$.  At inference
time, the cost is identical to a standard matrix-vector product
$\mW_{\mathrm{fused}} \, \vx$ in $\mathcal{O}(mn)$.  $\qed$
\end{proof}

\section{Entropy Estimation Details}
\label{app:entropy}

We estimate activation entropy via histogram binning with $B = 256$ bins over
the dynamic range $[\min(\hat{x}), \max(\hat{x})]$.  For numerical stability
we add $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$ before taking logarithms.  The estimate is
consistent (converges to the true entropy as the number of samples grows) and
computationally inexpensive: a single pass over the flattened tensor.

\section{Triton Kernel Pseudocode}
\label{app:triton}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:triton} shows the structure of the ADAMA fused kernel for
the scan + Hadamard + output-projection chain.

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
  \caption{ADAMA Fused Triton Kernel (simplified)}
  \label{alg:triton}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require $x \in \RR^{B \times L \times D}$, $\mW_{\mathrm{fused}}$,
             tile size $T$ (from Eq.~\eqref{eq:tile-size})
    \For{each programme id $(b, \ell_{\mathrm{start}})$}
      \State Load $x[b, \ell_{\mathrm{start}}:\ell_{\mathrm{start}}+T, :]$ into SRAM
      \State Execute selective scan update in SRAM
      \Comment{$\vy = \bar{A}h + \bar{B}x$}
      \State Apply WHT butterfly in SRAM
      \Comment{$\tilde{\vy} = \mH \vy / \sqrt{D}$}
      \State $\text{out} \leftarrow \mW_{\mathrm{fused}} \cdot \tilde{\vy}$
      \Comment{absorbed rotation; no extra load/store}
      \State Store out to HBM
    \EndFor
  \end{algorithmic}
