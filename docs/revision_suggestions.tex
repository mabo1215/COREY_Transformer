\section*{Summary of Recommendation}

\textbf{Major Revision (Aggregate Score: 63/100).}
This is a fresh independent review conducted after the prior reviewer
cycle (issues C1--C8, M1--M9) has been fully addressed.  The reviewers
acknowledge substantial progress: the paper now provides genuine
two-tier GPU evidence (Tier-1 cost-model diagnostics in appendix;
Tier-2 real Triton kernel benchmark in main text), a correctly included
Static-256 oracle, honest disclosure of Theorem~1's falsification on
real checkpoints, and removal of the ``no quality regression'' tautology.

\textbf{Four issues block acceptance at NeurIPS 2026 in the current form:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The NeurIPS~2026 mandatory checklist is absent from the submission.
  \item The practical value of entropy guidance over static tuning is
        asserted but not demonstrated: no experiment shows that entropy
        varies enough across real inference workloads to require runtime
        measurement rather than one-time profiling.
  \item The Hadamard reparameterization layer is theoretically motivated
        but has zero measured experimental results.
  \item The theoretical motivation for entropy-guided scheduling is
        internally inconsistent after Theorem~1's falsification, and the
        two entropy signals (input entropy for scheduling vs.\ entropy
        gain from Hadamard) are conflated in the paper narrative.
\end{enumerate}

All four issues are addressable.  The fundamental contribution---runtime
entropy-guided chunk-size selection that automates per-model
tuning---is genuine and would benefit the community if the evidence for
its practical utility is strengthened.

\section{Major Issues}

% STAGE: 10 (revision)

\begin{majorbox}
\textbf{[N1] Entropy variance across real inference workloads is
uncharacterized. (EIC, R1, DA --- CONSENSUS-3 / DA-CRITICAL)}

\textbf{Problem.}
Table~1 (\texttt{tab:signal\_chain\_tier2}) contains a single entropy
observation: $H = 4.18$~nats for a 512-token NarrativeQA prompt on
Mamba-370M, yielding tile recommendation 288.  The value 4.18~nats is
near the theoretical maximum for $K = 64$ bins ($\log 64 \approx
4.16$~nats), indicating near-maximum-uniform activations.  The paper's
practical contribution is ``automatic chunk-size selection without
manual oracle tuning per model or sequence length.''  However:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item One observation is insufficient to characterize whether entropy
        varies across prompts, tasks, sequence lengths, or model sizes.
  \item If real-world inference consistently yields near-maximum entropy
        (as this single data point suggests), a fixed large static chunk
        (e.g., chunk~$=512$ or chunk~$=256$) achieves the same
        performance without any runtime measurement.
  \item No experiment shows a scenario where entropy falls below a
        threshold and COREY correctly reduces chunk size, preventing
        a quality or performance problem.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Required action.}
Report entropy distribution statistics across at least 50 real-inference
prompts drawn from at least two LongBench tasks (e.g., NarrativeQA and
Qasper) for Mamba-370M.  Report mean, standard deviation, and p5/p95
percentiles.  Show what fraction of prompts falls in each
chunk-recommendation bucket (32, 64, 128, 256, 512).  If the
distribution is highly concentrated near maximum entropy, revise the
contribution claim to be more specific about the regime where dynamic
selection is beneficial.  If entropy shows meaningful variation, present
a case where the adaptive behavior of COREY leads to better performance
than a single fixed large chunk.
\end{majorbox}

\begin{majorbox}
\textbf{[N2] Hadamard reparameterization has zero measured experimental
results. (EIC, R3 --- CONSENSUS-2)}

\textbf{Problem.}
The Hadamard layer (Section~3.2, Theorem~3) is the second major
contribution of the paper.  The forward computation absorbs
$W' = W\mathbf{H}^\top$ and operates in the rotated basis.
Theorem~3 proves a $\sqrt{d}$ peak-coordinate reduction, directly
relevant to clipping-limited quantization.  However:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The Tier-2 entropy hook is \emph{passive}: it observes
        activations but does not apply Hadamard to the forward
        computation.
  \item No experiment measures perplexity with versus without the
        Hadamard layer.
  \item No quantization experiment (W8A8/W4A8) is reported anywhere.
  \item ``Empirical implication'' for Theorem~3 (Section~4.3) explicitly
        defers the analogous effect to future work.
\end{itemize}
The Hadamard contribution is theoretically present but experimentally
absent.  A NeurIPS systems paper is expected to validate its
contributions experimentally.

\textbf{Required action (Option A --- preferred).}
Implement the offline weight absorption ($W' = W\mathbf{H}^\top$,
a one-time O($d^2$) operation at model load time) for Mamba-370M.  Run
inference with this forward-modifying variant and report perplexity
(WikiText-103 or PG19, 20+ samples) vs.\ the unmodified baseline.  Even
a null result (identical perplexity) would be informative and is
sufficient to confirm functional equivalence.

\textbf{Required action (Option B --- if Option A is blocked).}
Remove the Hadamard layer as a claimed contribution; retain Theorem~3
as a theoretical property and note it as a design target for future
work.  Revise the title and abstract to reflect that the current system
contribution is chunk-size scheduling only.
\end{majorbox}

\begin{majorbox}
\textbf{[N3] The two entropy signals are conflated, creating a
misleading theoretical narrative after Theorem~1's falsification.
(R2, DA --- CONSENSUS-2 / DA-CRITICAL)}

\textbf{Problem.}
The paper uses two distinct entropy signals:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Signal A (scheduling):} The entropy of \emph{input}
        activations $\widetilde{H}(\text{input})$, used by COREY's
        online hook to select chunk size.
  \item \textbf{Signal B (Hadamard motivation):} The entropy
        \emph{gain} after Hadamard rotation
        $\Delta H = H(\mathbf{H}z) - H(z)$, motivated by Theorem~1.
\end{itemize}
These are different quantities with different empirical behavior:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Signal A (input entropy) is near maximum on real checkpoints
        ($H \approx 4.18$~nats from Table~1).
  \item Signal B (entropy gain) is \emph{negative} on real checkpoints
        (mean $-1.40 \pm 0.37$~nats from Remark~1).
\end{itemize}
The paper correctly discloses this in Remark~1, but the narrative in
Sections~1, 3, and 4 still tells a unified story: Hadamard increases
entropy~$\rightarrow$ enables deeper fusion~$\rightarrow$ COREY
measures entropy and fuses aggressively.  After Theorem~1's
falsification, this unified story is misleading for real-checkpoint
applications.

\textbf{Required action.}
Add an explicit paragraph (or subsection) that names Signal~A and
Signal~B as separate, explains that Signal~A is what COREY uses in
practice, explains that Theorem~1 concerns Signal~B and is empirically
limited to the synthetic prototype regime, and provides an independent
(informal or formal) justification for why high Signal~A (high input
entropy) predicts safe large chunk sizes.  The justification for
Signal~A may be straightforward (uniform activations mean predictable
memory access patterns, reducing the risk of register spill from
uneven data) but it should be stated explicitly and not derived via
Theorem~1.
\end{majorbox}

\begin{majorbox}
\textbf{[N4] COREY selects chunk~$=256$ for near-maximum entropy; the
oracle is chunk~$=512$ (53.4\% faster). The conservative selection
is unjustified for the tested uniform-random activations. (R1, DA ---
CONSENSUS-2)}

\textbf{Problem.}
Appendix Table~\texttt{tab:chunk\_sweep} shows chunk~$=512$ is 53.4\%
faster than chunk~$=256$ for the benchmark configuration.  COREY selects
chunk~$=256$ for $H \approx 4.18$~nats (near-maximum entropy ---
maximally uniform activations).  The paper justifies this as ``conservative
selection to avoid numerical error over the recurrent state when
activations are non-uniform.''  However:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The tested activations are uniformly random (not non-uniform).
  \item Near-maximum entropy is precisely the regime where the paper's
        own reasoning suggests chunk~$=512$ is safe.
  \item No experiment demonstrates numerical error at chunk~$=512$ for
        any tested configuration.
\end{itemize}
This means COREY's formula does not select the optimal chunk for the
tested configuration, and the reason (``avoid numerical error on
non-uniform activations'') does not apply to the uniform-random test
case.

\textbf{Required action (Option A --- preferred).}
Revise the chunk-size selection formula to select chunk~$=512$ for
near-maximum entropy inputs, or add a resource constraint (register
pressure, shared-memory limit) that explains why chunk~$=512$ is
infeasible for the tested configuration.  Show the revised COREY result
in Table~2.

\textbf{Required action (Option B).}
Provide an experiment showing that chunk~$=512$ causes measurable
numerical error (e.g., increased perplexity, NaN states) for some
activation regime, and show that COREY's entropy estimate correctly
predicts which regime requires smaller chunks.
\end{majorbox}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{[M1] Table~1 is a single data row and does not justify a formal
table. (DA, EIC)}

\texttt{tab:signal\_chain\_tier2} has one data row:
``Default (policy\_corey): 4.18 nats, Tile rec.\ 288.''  A single
observation requires at most an inline statement, not a formal table.
Replace with a sentence in the text, and use the table slot for the
entropy distribution data requested in N1.
\end{minorbox}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{[M2] ``No-Fusion'' latency baseline (403--502~ms) measures
Python dispatch overhead, not hardware compute limits. (DA, R1)}

The $364\text{--}365\times$ speedup vs.\ No-Fusion is correctly labeled
in the caption as ``Python dispatch overhead,'' but appears in the first
row of the headline results table.  Readers scanning Table~2 will focus
on this number.  Either:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Move No-Fusion to a footnote or separate table; or
  \item Replace No-Fusion with a \emph{single-chunk Triton call per
        timestep} (chunk~$=1$, implemented as a Triton call with
        batch~$=1$) to provide a fair hardware-level unfused baseline.
\end{itemize}
\end{minorbox}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{[M3] Scope the title and abstract to Mamba-1.x rather than
generic ``Selective State Space Models.'' (R2)}

The paper evaluates exclusively on Mamba-1.x checkpoints
(\texttt{state-spaces/mamba-370m-hf}, 1.4b, 2.8b).  Mamba-2's SSD
scan has fundamentally different hardware characteristics.  Using
``Selective State Space Models'' in the title implies broader coverage.
Revise to ``Mamba Selective Scan Kernels'' or ``Mamba-Family SSMs'' to
avoid misleading readers about scope.
\end{minorbox}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{[M4] Add a paragraph comparing runtime entropy guidance to
one-time profiling calibration. (DA)}

A practitioner could run a 30-second chunk-size sweep (as in
Appendix~\texttt{tab:chunk\_sweep}) to find the optimal static chunk
without any inference overhead.  The paper does not compare this
alternative.  Add a ``Comparison with static profiling'' paragraph in
Section~6 or the Limitations, explaining the trade-offs: runtime entropy
guidance requires no separate profiling pass (good for deployment
diversity across models/hardware/prompts) but adds per-forward-pass
measurement overhead (currently negligible per Table~3).  State clearly
in which scenarios runtime measurement is preferable to static
calibration.
\end{minorbox}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{[M5] LongBench task scores with exact-match~$= 0$ for all
models should be removed from or clarified in the main text. (R2)}

Table~3 (Appendix) reports ``MF-EN exact-match is zero across all
models due to strict exact-match evaluation on 32-token generation.''
These scores confirm harness execution but do not characterize model
quality.  Remove these columns from the main-text description or add
an explicit note that the zero scores are an evaluation-configuration
artifact unrelated to model capability.
\end{minorbox}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{[M6] Entropy EMA convergence time is uncharacterized. (R3)}

With EMA decay $\lambda = 0.85$, the running estimate requires
approximately $1/(1-\lambda) = 6.7$ steps to reflect recent
activations.  For short prompts (e.g., 8-token inputs), the estimate
may be dominated by initialization.  Add a note in the appendix
describing the convergence behavior and any minimum prompt length
required for a stable recommendation.
\end{minorbox}

\section{Suggested Revisions for Camera-Ready}

\begin{suggestbox}
\textbf{[S1] Entropy variance experiment.}

For the key experiment requested in N1, the following minimal design
suffices:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Run Mamba-370M on 50 prompts from each of NarrativeQA and
        Qasper (standard LongBench subsets).
  \item Log the entropy hook value ($H$~nats) at the first \texttt{in\_proj}
        output of layer 0 for each prompt.
  \item Report the distribution: histogram, mean, std, p5/p95.
  \item Annotate the chunk-size thresholds on the histogram.
  \item If the distribution shows variance spanning multiple threshold
        buckets, present one example where entropy correctly triggered
        a smaller chunk and compare latency/accuracy to a fixed
        large-chunk baseline.
\end{enumerate}
\end{suggestbox}

\begin{suggestbox}
\textbf{[S2] Forward-modifying Hadamard proof-of-concept.}

A minimal Hadamard experiment:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Compute $W' = W\mathbf{H}^\top$ offline for the
        \texttt{in\_proj} layer of Mamba-370M.
  \item Run WikiText-103 perplexity (20 samples) with the
        modified weight and the original weight.
  \item Report whether perplexity is preserved (expected: yes, by
        functional equivalence), and whether generation outputs match
        exactly.
\end{enumerate}
This experiment is low-cost (only one weight matrix to modify per layer)
and would definitively validate functional equivalence, turning
Theorem~3 from a theoretical claim into a verified implementation
property.
\end{suggestbox}

\begin{suggestbox}
\textbf{[S3] Reframe Table~2 to lead with COREY's key value.}

Rewrite the headline result sentence to explicitly state:
\begin{quote}
``COREY's value is in selecting chunk~$=256$ automatically from the
measured activation entropy ($H = 4.18$~nats), matching the
performance of Static-256 without manual per-model or per-workload
profiling.  On configurations where entropy correctly predicts
suboptimal chunk sizes, COREY adapts while a fixed static baseline
cannot.''
\end{quote}
Then add the entropy distribution evidence (N1/S1) to substantiate the
second sentence.
\end{suggestbox}

\begin{suggestbox}
\textbf{[S4] Separate contribution claims clearly.}

Revise Section~1 (Contributions) to list three clearly separate
contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Chunk-size scheduling:} An entropy-driven runtime policy
        for selecting selective-scan chunk size, validated with real GPU
        kernel timing.
  \item \textbf{Hadamard layer design:} A fused Hadamard
        reparameterization that preserves functional equivalence while
        redistributing activation energy; theoretically validated
        (Theorem~3); forward-modifying experiment in preparation.
  \item \textbf{Entropy--fusion co-design theory:} Formal analysis
        connecting entropy gain, fusion depth bounds, and quantization
        stability, with explicit scope conditions for the synthetic
        prototype regime vs.\ real checkpoints.
\end{enumerate}
This separation makes it clear which contribution has full experimental
support (1), which is at the proof-of-concept stage (2), and which is
theoretical (3).
\end{suggestbox}

\section*{Recommendation Rationale}

COREY addresses a real problem in SSM inference efficiency and the
authors have demonstrated genuine scientific integrity through multiple
revision cycles.  The core scheduling idea is sound and the Tier-2
evidence is legitimate.  The gap between the current state and NeurIPS
acceptance is the missing evidence that (a) entropy varies in practice
across real workloads, and (b) this variation makes static tuning
inadequate.  Both gaps are closable with modest additional
experimentation.

The paper would benefit most from:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item An entropy distribution plot across real prompts (N1/S1).
  \item A forward-modifying Hadamard proof of concept (N2/S2).
  \item Cleaner narrative separation of the two entropy signals (N3).
  \item Addition of the NeurIPS 2026 checklist (V1).
\end{enumerate}

With these additions, the paper presents a coherent and novel
contribution at the intersection of activation statistics and memory
system scheduling for SSM inference, suitable for NeurIPS 2026.
