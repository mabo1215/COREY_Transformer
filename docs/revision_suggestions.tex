\section*{4. Remaining Issues}

\subsection*{R1. The Conclusion overstates the Tier-2a advance}

The Conclusion states that the real-checkpoint evaluation ``closes the passive-hook limitation
through an active-mode integration.'' While technically accurate (entropy now runs inline on
the critical path), this phrasing implies more closure than the paper delivers: the selected
chunk size is still not routed into the scan kernel, so no end-to-end latency benefit is
demonstrated on real checkpoints. A reviewer who reads this sentence before the Scope of
Claims will have inflated expectations.

\textbf{Fix:} Rewrite the Conclusion sentence to say that Tier-2a closes the
\emph{monitoring path} (inline entropy with measured overhead), while Tier-2b closes the
\emph{performance path} (kernel speedup), and that routing the two together is the
remaining engineering step.

\subsection*{R2. The ``comparison with static profiling'' paragraph partially undermines the contribution}

The paragraph at the end of Section~6.2 correctly notes that a practitioner could replace
runtime entropy guidance with a one-time offline sweep.  However, the framing suggests that
offline profiling may be preferable in stable deployments, which raises the question of why
a reviewer should care about the runtime signal at all.

\textbf{Fix:} Reframe this paragraph as a concrete characterization of COREY's advantage
envelope: (a)~COREY eliminates the offline sweep on workloads where entropy is stable,
providing the same chunk decision automatically; (b)~COREY remains the correct choice when
the activation distribution cannot be profiled in advance (new hardware targets, mixed
workloads, continuous fine-tuning); (c)~offline profiling is the right choice only when
deployment conditions are fixed and the sweep cost is amortized. The current text makes
case~(c) too prominent relative to cases~(a) and~(b).

\subsection*{R3. Abstract is dense and slightly front-loaded with overhead numbers}

The abstract is ~200 words and leads with detailed latency measurements
($1160.9\pm12.0$\,ms, $1257.5\pm30.3$\,ms) before the conceptual contribution is
stated. This ordering makes the abstract read as an engineering report rather than
a research contribution abstract. The NeurIPS style guide advises leading with the problem,
contribution, and main finding, with measurements as supporting evidence.

\textbf{Fix:} Reorder the abstract to: (1)~problem statement, (2)~COREY concept and
Tier-1 result, (3)~key Tier-2 findings (inline overhead + kernel speedup), (4)~Concept \&
Feasibility positioning. Move the raw latency numbers after the speedup numbers.

\subsection*{R4. The ``COREY (calibrated)'' row borrows the Static-512 latency figure}

The footnote (§) correctly explains that the 0.748\,ms figure for COREY~(calibrated) is
``reused from the separately measured Static-512 row (same kernel, same chunk size).''
However, readers who scan the table without reading the footnote will interpret this as a
directly measured COREY number. The current table formatting does not visually distinguish
this row from the measured rows.

\textbf{Fix:} Add an italic or asterisk marker to the latency and std columns for the
COREY~(calibrated) row to signal that these are shared with the Static-512 row, not
independently measured COREY runs. The footnote text is correct; the table formatting
should reinforce it.

\subsection*{R5. Evaluation breadth remains the primary barrier to a strong accept}

The LongBench evaluation covers four tasks at 20 samples each, and only one external
baseline model (Pythia-410M as an architectural sanity-check) is included.
There is no Mamba-2, RWKV-6, or FlashAttention Transformer comparison.
This is acknowledged in Limitations Section~6, and the Concept \& Feasibility
positioning mitigates it, but it remains the single largest gap for a NeurIPS
main-track reader.

\textbf{Status:} Genuinely blocked by infrastructure (no A100/H100, Mamba-2 architectural
incompatibility). This item cannot be closed in the current environment.
\textit{Recommendation}: retain the current Limitations note but add a one-sentence
forward pointer in the Conclusion to the specific experiment type that would turn
this into a full systems contribution (e.g., ``A direct comparison of COREY against
Static-512 on a unified 4096-token Mamba-370M LongBench run with a matched Mamba-2
kernel would close this gap.'').

\subsection*{R6. Section structure creates one redundant appendix forward-reference in Section~7}

The Ablation Studies section (Section~7) consists entirely of a forward reference to the
appendix: ``All five prototype ablation studies...are reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_ablations}.''
The section provides no new main-text content. This creates an awkward section that is
essentially a single-sentence pointer.

\textbf{Fix:} Either (a)~inline the single key ablation finding (entropy term independently
contributes 21.6--22.7\% latency reduction beyond arithmetic intensity alone) directly
into the Ablation Studies section body without removing the appendix reference, or
(b)~merge this section into the Results discussion.

\section*{7. Suggested Revisions}

\subsection*{T1. Fix Conclusion Tier-2a framing (R1)}
% STAGE: 10 (revision)
In the Conclusion paragraph beginning ``Our real-checkpoint evaluation'', replace the phrase
``closes the passive-hook limitation through an active-mode integration'' with language that
accurately characterizes Tier-2a as closing the \emph{inline monitoring path} and defers the
\emph{speedup path} to future work. Example: ``Our Tier-2a evaluation advances beyond passive
monitoring: entropy computation and chunk selection now execute inline on the critical path
of \texttt{model.generate()}, with a measured $8.3\%$ end-to-end overhead. Routing the
selected chunk into the scan kernel---which would realize the $3.24\times$ speedup from
Tier-2b---remains the next engineering step.''

\subsection*{T2. Reframe the static-profiling comparison paragraph (R2)}
% STAGE: 10 (revision)
In Section~6.2, replace the current paragraph starting ``A practitioner could replace runtime
entropy guidance with a one-time chunk sweep'' with a three-case framing: (a)~stable deployment
where offline profiling is amortized (offline is also fine), (b)~variable deployment conditions
where runtime entropy provides automatic adaptation, (c)~COREY (calibrated) eliminates the
sweep entirely in the medium-entropy regime. Frame cases~(b) and~(c) as the primary motivation.

\subsection*{T3. Reorder abstract (R3)}
% STAGE: 10 (revision)
Move the raw timing numbers ($1160.9\pm12.0$\,ms, $1257.5\pm30.3$\,ms) to appear after the
$3.24\times$ / $4.41\times$ speedup numbers in the abstract. The ordering should be:
problem $\to$ concept $\to$ Tier-1 summary $\to$ Tier-2b speedup $\to$ Tier-2a overhead
$\to$ Concept \& Feasibility positioning.

\subsection*{T4. Add italic formatting to COREY (calibrated) latency columns (R4)}
% STAGE: 10 (revision)
In Table~1 (\texttt{tab:w1\_chunked\_scan}), wrap the latency and std values for the
``COREY (calibrated)'' row in \textit{italic} to visually distinguish them from independently
measured values. Ensure the footnote (§) already present makes this explicit.

\subsection*{T5. Add key finding to Ablation Studies section (R6)}
% STAGE: 10 (revision)
In Section~7 (Ablation Studies), add one sentence inline before the appendix reference:
``The combined entropy-plus-arithmetic-intensity signal achieves 21.6--22.7\% latency
reduction over no-fusion, exceeding the 17.5\% from arithmetic intensity alone and
confirming that the entropy term provides independent discriminative power; full tables
are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_ablations}.''

\subsection*{T6. Add forward pointer for evaluation breadth gap (R5)}

In the Conclusion's final paragraph, add one sentence specifying the exact experiment type
