\section{Overall Assessment}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Recommendation:} \emph{Reject (3 -- below the acceptance threshold).
Strong revision required before resubmission.}

\textbf{Summary confidence:} High. The paper has been read in full, including
the appendix, the \texttt{.tex} sources, and the supplementary bibliography.

\subsection*{Summary}

COREY proposes combining entropy-guided operator fusion with a fused Hadamard
reparameterization for selective state space models (SSMs, specifically the
Mamba family). Activation entropy, estimated via fixed-width histograms with
exponential moving-average smoothing, is used as a scheduling signal to place
kernel fusion boundaries and to select tile sizes. A normalized Hadamard matrix
is absorbed into linear projections to reduce activation outlier concentration,
raising post-rotation entropy and thereby expanding the safe fusion depth. Three
formal results are provided: (i) an entropy growth theorem under doubly-stochastic
histogram mixing, (ii) a shared-memory fusion depth bound, and (iii) a
peak-coordinate clipping bound under orthonormal rotation. Experiments are
conducted exclusively on a CPU-only, NumPy/PyTorch prototype. Checkpoint-level
experiments on Mamba-370M/1.4B/2.8B are present in Appendix D but are explicitly
flagged as smoke tests that do not use the proposed fused kernels and hence cannot
validate the main claims.

\subsection*{Main Problems in Brief}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{P\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2.5em]

  \item \textbf{Prototype-only evaluation.}
  Every main-table number (latency, throughput, DRAM bytes/token) is produced
  by a Python-level simulation without any actual GPU kernel invocation. The
  paper itself acknowledges in at least eight separate limitation bullets that
  ``full checkpoint-level deployment experiments remain future work.'' This
  fundamentally limits the paper's ability to substantiate its primary systems
  claims at the level expected by NeurIPS.

  \item \textbf{Circular quality proxy.}
  The low-bit ``diagnostic proxy'' is hand-crafted to reward entropy gain and
  outlier reduction---the very quantities COREY optimizes. Evaluating with a
  self-referential metric is not a substitute for perplexity, downstream task
  accuracy, or hardware-measured quality degradation.

  \item \textbf{Incomplete ablation coverage.}
  Ablation tables provide only three $\tau$ values and speedup ratios, but
  provide no data tables for tile-size or bit-width ablations referenced in the
  text. Tiling and quantization ablations are described only in prose without
  any accompanying numbers.

  \item \textbf{Duplicated paragraph in Appendix D.}
  Appendix D contains a near-verbatim duplicated paragraph describing the
  checkpoint matrix status (``single-checkpoint pipeline verified'' \emph{vs.}
  ``two-checkpoint matrix partially populated''), indicating incomplete revision
  management that must be resolved before resubmission.

  \item \textbf{Weak novelty justification.}
  Using entropy as a scheduling heuristic and Hadamard smoothing for quantization
  are both established ideas. The paper does not provide a clear empirical or
  theoretical argument for why their combination in the context of SSM fusion
  constitutes a contribution beyond their independent prior uses.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Detailed Critique by Dimension}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Novelty and Significance}

The core observation---that Hadamard rotation raises histogram entropy and that
higher entropy is correlated with safer, deeper fusion---is conceptually
appealing. However, the paper's contribution relative to prior art requires
sharper delineation.

\begin{itemize}

  \item Hadamard smoothing for SSM quantization has been explicitly studied in
  QuaRot, Quamba~\cite{chiang2024quamba}, and MambaQuant~\cite{xu2025mambaquant}.
  The paper cites these works but does not empirically demonstrate that its
  mechanism of using post-rotation entropy as a \emph{fusion scheduler} signal
  is the correct or superior design choice compared to, e.g., using arithmetic
  intensity alone.

  \item The entropy-guided scheduling idea is closely related to profiling-driven
  compilation pipelines (e.g., TVM, XLA). The paper should discuss why a
  runtime entropy signal is preferable to a static compile-time profile for SSM
  workloads.

  \item The dynamic programming formulation in Appendix B is standard
  interval-DP applied to a 1D chain. The novelty here is marginal unless the
  utility function $U(R)$ itself is shown to produce substantially different
  partitions than arithmetic-intensity-only scheduling.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Technical Correctness}

The three theorems are mathematically sound as stated, but the gap between the
theorems and the deployed system is wide.

\begin{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Theorem 4.1} (Entropy Growth): The result is correct and the
  full proof in Appendix C.1 is rigorous. However, the paper itself notes that
  the doubly-stochastic mixing assumption ``is conditional'' and that the theorem
  does not claim every Hadamard rotation satisfies it. No empirical evidence is
  provided that the assumption holds for actual Mamba activation distributions.
  The theorem therefore serves as motivation rather than a guarantee, which is
  acceptable but should be stated more prominently in the main text.

  \item \textbf{Theorem 4.2} (Depth Bound): The bound $F \leq M_{\mathrm{shared}}
  / C_{\mathrm{tile}}$ is dimensionally trivial---it follows directly from the
  definition of feasibility. It does not account for the interaction between tile
  shape, occupancy, and register pressure, all of which are enumerated in
  Eq.~(A.2) of the appendix. Calling this a theorem overstates its technical
  content.

  \item \textbf{Theorem 4.3} (Quantization Stability): The bound
  $\|z\|_\infty \leq \|x\|_1 / \sqrt{d}$ is correct for a normalized Hadamard
  matrix. The one-outlier example $x = M e_r$ is a useful illustration. However,
  for realistic activations with many moderate-magnitude entries, $\|x\|_1$ may
  be $O(d \sigma)$, which makes the bound loose by $O(\sqrt{d})$ and potentially
  vacuous compared to the original $\|x\|_\infty = O(\sigma)$ regime. The paper
  should discuss the tightness regime.

  \item The entropy EMA in Eq.~(1) accumulates a \emph{scalar} running estimate
  for the prototype, yet the tile-mapping function $T(e)$ in Appendix B.5 treats
  $e$ as a value in $[0,8]$ (raw histogram entropy), while the main experiments
  use normalized thresholds in $[0.45, 0.60]$. The two scales are inconsistent
  and never reconciled in the paper. This is a presentation error that could
  confuse reproducibility.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Adequacy}

This is the central weakness of the submission.

\begin{itemize}

  \item \textbf{No real GPU kernel.} All main experiments run on a CPU-only
  Python prototype. Latency and throughput figures measure Python-level timing of
  a scheduler simulation, not actual memory-bandwidth savings on a GPU. The
  magnitude and even the direction of speedups in a real Triton or CUDA kernel
  can differ substantially from a simulation, because register spilling, warp
  occupancy, and L1/L2 hit rates all interact in ways the prototype does not
  model.

  \item \textbf{Synthetic-only activations.} The prototype uses hidden dimension
  192 and projection dimension 256---substantially smaller than Mamba-370M (which
  uses $d_{\text{model}} = 512$) or Mamba-1.4B ($d_{\text{model}} = 2048$). The
  paper provides no sensitivity analysis showing that the entropy-guided policy
  generalizes across dimension scales.

  \item \textbf{Self-referential quality proxy.} The low-bit diagnostic proxy
  is a linear combination of entropy gain, outlier reduction, and a precision
  sensitivity term, all of which directly reward the proposed method by
  construction. Table 3 shows that this proxy is \emph{zero} for Entropy-Guided
  Fusion but positive for Static Fusion, which is trivially guaranteed by the
  proxy's design. This result carries no empirical information content.

  \item \textbf{Incomplete ablation tables.} Section 7 describes four ablations
  (threshold sweep, tiling policy, bit width, sequence stratification), but only
  two of them---threshold sweep (Table 3) and sequence stratification
  (Table 4)---are backed by numerical data. The tiling and bit-width ablations
  are text-only. Missing tables should be added.

  \item \textbf{Checkpoint evidence does not validate the method.}
  Appendix D reports Mamba-370M/1.4B/2.8B benchmark results from a WSL2
  fast-path environment, but these runs use only the \emph{baseline} HuggingFace
  inference path---no static-fusion or COREY variant has been executed on any
  real checkpoint. The appendix results therefore validate the harness, not the
  method.

  \item \textbf{No wall-clock variance or statistical testing.} Tables 1--4
  report point estimates only. The paper states that 5 repeated runs are used,
  but no standard deviations, confidence intervals, or significance tests are
  reported for the main tables (only Appendix D checkpoint runs include standard
  deviations). The claimed speedups of 5--25\% are small relative to typical
  run-to-run variance in timed Python benchmarks.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Writing Clarity and Presentation}

The paper is generally well-written and is careful about scope. However, several
presentation issues must be addressed.

\begin{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Duplicated paragraph in Appendix D.} Lines 234--242 and
  lines 225--233 of the appendix source describe checkpoint validation status
  with nearly identical text, one labeled ``two-checkpoint matrix partially
  populated'' and the other ``three-checkpoint benchmark matrix populated.'' This
  is clearly an artifact of incremental revision. One paragraph must be deleted.

  \item \textbf{Entropy scale inconsistency.} The prototype uses normalized
  entropy thresholds $\tau \in \{0.45, 0.52, 0.60\}$, while the checkpoint-side
  hook uses $\tau_0 = 5.0$ for raw histogram entropy. This discrepancy is
  explained in Appendix B.3 but should also be flagged in the main text to
  prevent reader confusion.

  \item \textbf{Table numbering.} The appendix replicates Table 1 from the main
  text verbatim (labeled Table 5 in Appendix B.5). This redundancy consumes page
  budget. The appendix table should be removed or replaced with a pointer.

  \item \textbf{Theorem numbering inconsistency.} Main-text theorems are labeled
  4.1, 4.2, 4.3 (by section). Appendix theorems restart as A.1, A.2, etc.
  Theorem 4.1 in the main text and Theorem A.1 in the appendix have identical
  statements, creating a confusing dual numbering for the same result.

  \item \textbf{Missing units in Table 2.} The ``Latency (ms)'' column header
  correctly carries units, but the ``TPS'' column does not clarify whether
  throughput is tokens/s. The ``DRAM Bytes/token'' column appears to report
  values in the thousands for short sequences (2805.42), which likely reflects
  simulated bytes in the prototype cost model rather than measured hardware
  bandwidth. The unit should be clarified.

  \item \textbf{Figure 1 axes.} The x-axis of Figure 1 uses powers of 2 ($2^{10}$
  through $2^{16}$), which is appropriate, but tick labels would be more readable
  with the sequence length also shown in plain integers (1024, 2048, \ldots).

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reproducibility}

The reproducibility checklist (Appendix A.6) is commendably detailed for the
prototype. However:

\begin{itemize}

  \item The prototype runs on a CPU without GPU involvement, so any future GPU
  reproduction will necessarily differ in timing by an unknown factor.

  \item The tile-mapping function $T(e)$ depends on $\min(e, 8)$, which clamps
  entropy at 8 nats. For normalized entropy in $[0,1]$ this clamp is never active,
  but for raw entropy ($K=256$ bins, max entropy $\approx 5.5$ nats) it similarly
  appears inactive. The authors should clarify under which scale $e$ is measured
  when $T(e)$ is invoked in the prototype.

  \item The code repository is not linked or described. NeurIPS reproducibility
  standards recommend anonymous repository access.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and Broader Impact}

The limitations section (Section 8) is unusually candid---eight separate
limitation bullets cover most of the issues raised above. While honesty is valued,
a limitations section this extensive signals that the work is not yet ready for a
top-tier systems venue. Specifically, limitations 4 and 5 (narrow checkpoint
coverage, no real-checkpoint COREY runs) are not minor caveats but fundamental
gaps in the validation chain.

The paper does not include a broader impact statement, which is required by NeurIPS.

\section{Prioritized Revision Recommendations}
% ============================================================

The following recommendations are ordered from most to least critical for
acceptance.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{R\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2.5em]

  \item \critical{Provide at least one end-to-end GPU kernel benchmark.}
  The most important revision is to implement and benchmark even a minimal
  Triton kernel for the fused Hadamard + projection + elementwise block on a
  real GPU, and to compare its wall-clock latency and memory bandwidth against
  the unfused baseline. Without this, the central systems claim---that entropy
  guided fusion reduces memory traffic on real hardware---is unverifiable. A
  single-model, single-sequence-length proof-of-concept would suffice for a
  revised submission, provided variance is reported.

  \item \critical{Replace or supplement the self-referential quality proxy.}
  The low-bit proxy as currently defined is circular. Either (a) use perplexity
  on a standard language modeling benchmark (WikiText-103 results are already
  collected in Appendix D and could be used for this purpose), or (b) report the
  outlier ratio $(\lvert x \rvert > 6\sigma)$ and clipping error independently
  of the scheduling signal, so that the metric is not constructed from the same
  quantities the scheduler optimizes.

  \item \critical{Remove or consolidate the duplicated paragraph in Appendix D.}
  The two paragraphs describing checkpoint validation status (one asserting
  ``three-checkpoint matrix populated,'' the other asserting ``two-checkpoint
  matrix partially populated'') must be reconciled into a single, accurate,
  up-to-date paragraph before resubmission. This is a correctness issue.

  \item \major{Add numerical tables for tiling and bit-width ablations.}
  Sections 7.2 and 7.3 describe ablations without tabular data. Add at least
  two tables: (a) latency and occupancy as a function of tile size under fixed
  sequence length and precision, comparing static vs.\ entropy-guided tiling;
  and (b) latency, DRAM bytes/token, and proxy across FP16, W8A8, W4A8 for
  each method.

  \item \major{Report variance for all main experimental results.}
  All entries in Tables 1--4 should include standard deviations (or 95\%
  confidence intervals) across the 5 repeated runs. Variance estimates are
  particularly important for the speedup claims in Table 4, where the
  differences between methods are in the range of 5--12\%.

  \item \major{Resolve entropy scale inconsistency between prototype and harness.}
  Clearly state in both the main text and the appendix whether $\tau$ and $e$
  are expressed as normalized (0--1) entropy or as raw nats. If the prototype
  and the checkpoint-side hook use different scales, justify why and explain how
  the adaptive formula $T(e)$ is calibrated for each.

  \item \major{Strengthen novelty argument for the entropy-as-scheduler design.}
  Add an experiment or analysis comparing entropy-based boundary selection
  against arithmetic-intensity-only boundary selection (i.e., set $\alpha = 0$
  in the score $S(R)$). This would isolate the incremental value of the entropy
  signal beyond what static profiling already provides.

  \item \major{Fix theorem numbering and remove duplicate appendix table.}
  Unify theorem numbering across the main text and appendix (e.g., use
  Theorem 1, 2, 3 throughout, or cross-reference the appendix theorem as a
  ``Full Proof of Theorem 4.1''). Remove Table 5 in Appendix B.5, which
  duplicates Table 1 from the main text.

  \item \minor{Add standard deviations to Appendix D checkpoint tables and
  harmonize prompt lengths.} The WSL2 benchmark results use different prompt
  lengths for each model scale (8192 for 370M, 4096 for 1.4B, 2048 for 2.8B),
  making cross-model comparison meaningless. Fix to a common prompt length.

  \item \minor{Add a broader impact statement} as required by NeurIPS. At
  minimum, comment on energy consumption of SSM inference and whether
  COREY's efficiency gains have an environmental benefit.

  \item \minor{Clarify Figure 1 x-axis.} Add plain-integer tick annotations
  alongside the power-of-two labels. Clarify whether ``normalized entropy'' is
  divided by $\log K$ (the maximum possible entropy for $K$ bins) or by some
  other reference.

  \item \minor{Discuss tightness of Theorem 4.3.} Add a remark noting that when
  activations have $D$ non-negligible entries of magnitude $\sigma$, the bound
  gives $\|z\|_\infty \leq D\sigma / \sqrt{d}$, which is loose for $D = O(d)$.
  Clarify in which regime (small $D$, i.e., truly sparse outliers) the bound is
  informative.

\end{enumerate}

\section*{Revision Suggestions for COREY (NeurIPS 2026)}

\subsection*{I. Overall Verdict}

The paper presents a conceptually motivated framework (COREY) that unifies
entropy-guided operator fusion with Hadamard-based activation reparameterization
for selective SSMs. The theoretical analysis is sound and the prototype study is
reproducible. However, the submission falls below NeurIPS acceptance standards on
three fundamental dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item All efficiency results are produced by a Python-level prototype without
        any actual GPU kernel execution, making the core systems claims unverifiable.
  \item The quality metric for low-bit experiments is hand-crafted to favor the
        proposed method by construction.
  \item The appendix contains a duplicated, internally contradictory paragraph
        describing the checkpoint validation status.
\end{enumerate}

We strongly encourage the authors to revise and resubmit once at least a
minimal GPU kernel proof-of-concept is available and the evaluation methodology
is strengthened.

\subsection*{II. Critical Revisions (Must Address)}

\paragraph{C1. End-to-end GPU kernel benchmark (Priority: Critical).}
Implement and evaluate a Triton kernel for the core fused Hadamard + projection +
elementwise stage and report wall-clock latency, effective memory bandwidth, and
occupancy relative to the unfused baseline. Even a single model / single sequence
length data point on a real GPU would substantially strengthen the paper.

\paragraph{C2. Circular quality proxy (Priority: Critical).}
The low-bit diagnostic proxy is defined as a linear combination of entropy gain
and outlier reduction---the exact quantities COREY maximizes. Table 3 shows
entropy-guided fusion achieving a proxy of exactly 0.0000, which is guaranteed
by construction. Replace this metric with (a) real perplexity on WikiText-103
(already collected in the appendix), (b) independent clipping error measurement,
or (c) both.

\paragraph{C3. Duplicated paragraph in Appendix D (Priority: Critical).}
Lines approximately 562--570 and lines 571--579 of the appendix contain
near-identical text with contradictory claims (``three-checkpoint matrix
populated'' vs.\ ``two-checkpoint matrix partially populated''). One paragraph
must be deleted and the remaining paragraph must accurately reflect the current
state.

\subsection*{III. Major Revisions (Should Address)}

\paragraph{M1. Missing ablation tables.}
Sections 7.2 (tiling policy) and 7.3 (bit-width sensitivity) reference
comparisons that exist only as prose. Provide numerical tables for: (a) latency
and occupancy vs.\ tile size for static vs.\ entropy-guided tiling; (b) latency,
DRAM bytes/token, and quality metric across FP16, W8A8, W4A8.

\paragraph{M2. Variance reporting.}
Report standard deviations or 95\% confidence intervals for all entries in
Tables 1--4. This is especially important for Table 4, where speedup
differences between methods are in the 5--12\% range.

\paragraph{M3. Entropy scale inconsistency.}
The prototype uses normalized $\tau \in \{0.45, 0.52, 0.60\}$; the
checkpoint-side hook uses $\tau_0 = 5.0$ (raw nats). The tile-mapping function
$T(e) = 32\cdot\mathrm{round}\bigl(\frac{64 + \min(e,8)/8 \cdot 448}{32}\bigr)$
implies $e$ is in nats. Unify and document the entropy scale used in each
component and add a one-paragraph reconciliation to the main text.

\paragraph{M4. Ablation isolating entropy signal.}
Add a condition with $\alpha = 0$ (arithmetic-intensity-only scheduling) to the
ablation in Table 3 or a new table. This would empirically justify why entropy is
the correct scheduling signal rather than a simpler proxy already available from
kernel profiling.

\paragraph{M5. Theorem numbering and duplicate table.}
Unify theorem labels across the main body (Theorems 4.1--4.3) and the appendix
(Theorem A.1). The appendix proof should be titled ``Full Proof of Theorem 4.1''
rather than introducing a new label. Remove Table 5 in Appendix B.5, which
duplicates Table 1 from the main text verbatim.

\subsection*{IV. Minor Revisions (Should Address)}

\paragraph{m1. Harmonize checkpoint prompt lengths in Appendix D.}
Mamba-370M is benchmarked at 8192 tokens, Mamba-1.4B at 4096 tokens, and
Mamba-2.8B at 2048 tokens. Use a single common prompt length for all three
checkpoints to enable meaningful cross-model comparison.

\paragraph{m2. Add broader impact statement.}
NeurIPS requires a broader impact statement. Comment on the energy and carbon
implications of improved SSM inference efficiency.

\paragraph{m3. Clarify Figure 1 x-axis and entropy normalization.}
Add plain-integer tick labels (1024, 2048, \ldots) alongside power-of-two labels.
State explicitly whether normalized entropy is $H / \log K$ or another reference.

\paragraph{m4. Discuss Theorem 4.3 tightness.}
Add a remark noting that the bound $\|z\|_\infty \leq \|x\|_1/\sqrt{d}$ is
tight only in the one-outlier regime and may be loose by $O(\sqrt{d})$ for
activations with many moderate-magnitude entries.

\paragraph{m5. Link anonymous code repository.}
Provide an anonymous repository URL per NeurIPS reproducibility guidelines.
The reproducibility checklist in Appendix A.6 is otherwise one of the
strongest aspects of the submission.
